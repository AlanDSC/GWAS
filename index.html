Abstract

One of the greatest challenges in public health is the growing number of bacterial species that have developed resistance to antibiotics through point mutations in their genome. Our project aims to identify these genetic markers of antibiotic resistance through a genome wide association study between 36 antibiotic resistant E.Coli samples and 36 control samples. Genetic variants will be checked for statistical significance, and analyzed for any functional effects.

"By 2050, superbugs could kill 10 million people a year"

Although antibiotic resistant bacteria have been studied for decades, their whole genome sequencing has started relatively recently [3]. Due to their immunity to known medicine, their exposure to humans can lead to infections that are currently impossible to cure [1]. According to the International Federation of Pharmaceutical Manufacturers & Associations (IFPMA), antibiotic resistance will:

  • Force 28 million more people into poverty[2]
  • Cost the global economy up to 100 trillion dollars[2]
  • Kill up to 10 million people a year by 2050[2]

This study focuses on Enterobacteriaceae, specifically E. Coli, a common cause of infections, both in healthcare settings and communities [4]. Certain strains of E. Coli have developed an especially dangerous resistance mechanism, the ability to produce an enzyme known as extended-spectrum beta-lactamase, or ESBL [4]. ESBL is capable of breaking down multiple types of antibiotics such as penicillin, rendering them ineffective [4].

Understanding the genetic markers of antibiotic resistance can lead to better, more effective treatments to combat the growing concern of superbugs.

An Overview of the Pipeline

The Data

The study was conducted using two groups of samples, one with an ESBL-enzyme producing strain of E.Coli (denoted with DRR as the suffix), and a non-antibiotic resistant control group [5][6]. For each group, sample sizes were 36 whole genomes. The samples were obtained from Illumina NCBI's SRA Run Selector and formatted as .fastq files [5][6]. Each sample has two files associated with it, as they are paired-end reads, totalling to 144 files.

Quality Control

To ensure these 144 files will yield valid results later in the analysis, each was put through a quality control software called FastQC. FastQC takes in a single .fastq file and outputs a summary report listing basic statistics about the reads, including quality scores from base calling, the presence of adapter sequences, and percent N content, which indicates missing data.

While it’s important to check for quality of each sample individually, the FastQC results of our case and control groups were also aggregated in order to identify any major differences in quality between them that could affect the GWAS results. All 144 files passed the default FastQC check for basic statistics and adapter content, thus they were all included in the study. However, the antibiotic resistance group had more samples that had an unusually high per sequence GC content, indicated by the higher number of ESBL_Samples that failed this check (see GC and Adapter Content). This could be due to either the data being lower quality, OR this possibly being an actual physiological marker for genomes in this group.

Sequence Alignment, File Conversion, Sorting

A pair of .fastq files for one sample contain the raw reads from the entire genome, but those reads are essentially small fragments that need to be put together like a puzzle. This is where the process of alignment comes in. Using a previously known E. Coli reference genome, we utilized an alignment software called Bowtie2 which algorithmically pieces together these reads into a contiguous sequence. The output is a file in sam format, which is a text readable version of this sequence. Using samtools and picard, these sam files were then converted to .bam - a compressed version that runs more efficiently through bioinformatics software.

Variant Calling

The 72 cleaned and formatted .bam files then undergo Variant Calling, which is the process of identifying the Single Nucleotide Polymorphisms, or SNP’s in each sample. These are the point mutations that can differentiate samples, with the goal being able to find the SNP’s that are significantly different between our antibiotic-resistant and control groups. This was done using GATK’s HaplotypeCaller tool, which outputs a vcf that contains the list of variants found in a single sample. All of these vcf files were then merged into a single file using bcftools which can be used for our GWAS.

Results

PCA Analysis

So that was our entire process of data setup. We can move onto the actual “analysis” of these genomic variants, starting with a basic PCA to determine if there is any meaningful difference between the Antibiotic-resistant and Control groups. The principle components were calculated using Plink, and the scatterplot of the first two PC’s are shown on the right. The initial results look promising, with most of the antibiotic-resistant E. Coli samples being clustered together on the right. The control samples seem to have more variance, especially the three outliers in the top left. Because the sizes of our sample groups were already quite small, we chose to keep them in the study.

Genome Wide Association Study

In order to perform the GWAS, we used a Logistic Regression model which compares the two groups and calculates a p-value for each SNP. The model was further tuned by using the first two principal components as covariates, and all SNP’s with a genotyping rate of less than 60% were removed from the analysis. The genotyping rate was used as a measure of missing data, and we found that 60% was the best threshold to use in order to minimize the skew of our results.

Variant Annotation

To perform further analysis on our variants, we chose snpEff to help us with the variant annotation. We used the E-coli database that snpEff provided us as the reference in our annotation. This step basically gives us an annotated vcf file, a succinct summary report and gene_name table. We can then use snpSift to filter out the common variants which presented in both group and leave only the variants we need. This step helps us reduce the scope significantly and enable us to do more specific analyzation with it.
